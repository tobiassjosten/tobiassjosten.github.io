Michael Hyatt has been on my radar for a long time. His name comes up time and again in the personal development space, and I've always been curious about his work. Since I'm curious about personal branding and right now would be an opportune time to build my own platform, I decided to read his book on the subject.

Unfortunately, it was a bit of a disappointment. While it covers a lot of ground and has some useful insights, it doesn't really offer anything substantial in terms of strategy or ways of thinking about the topic, and the many tactical tips and advice it does offer are either common sense or very dated. Had I read it when it was published in 2012, I'm certain I would have found it useful, but in 2026, it feels too out of touch with the current landscape of personal branding and online platforms.

So no shade on Michael Hyatt, but I can't say I got much out of this book. I'm still looking to give his other, more current, reading a try, but this one didn't really hit the mark for me.

- A platform is the stage you build, from where you can speak, create, and connect. It's the foundation for your work and influence. The book argues that everyone needs a platform, and that building one is essential for success in the modern world.

- Michael Hyatt talks about ten elements required to create a "wow" experience: surprise, anticipation, resonance, transcendence, clarity, presence, universality, evangelism, longevity, privilege. These feel more cursory and descriptive than thoroughly prescriptive, though.

- I loved the example of the lobby experience, where Hyatt describes different scenarios of walking into a corporate lobby and how the experience can be designed to create a certain impression. It's a great metaphor for how attention to detail can shape the way people perceive you and your work, and it made me think about how we can design our own platforms to create a certain experience for our audience.

- The book contains so many lists. Lists of elements, lists of steps, lists of tips, lists of examples. The overwhelming amount of them added to the feeling of the book only scratching the surface of the topic, and that it was more like a collection of random thoughts than a cohesive strategy for building a platform.

- Part of the book is about product marketing, for which I would much rather read something by Alex Hormozi ($100M Offers and $100M Leads), who has a much more practical and actionable approach to the topic.

- Like many other authors, Michael Hyatt isn't only a writer but a prolific reader, and you can tell by the many references to other books and authors throughout the text. I appreciate the reading list I got from it!

- One positive tip I take away from the book is using one's email signature as a way to promote one's work and build one's platform. It's such a simple thing, but we know that repetition builds familiarity and this is basically a free, easy way to get your narrative and message in front of people on a regular basis. I will definitely be implementing this one.

- I liked how he conceptualizes the components of a platform as 1) a home base that you own, like a website or newsletter, 2) embassies, like social media profiles, and 3) outposts, like search monitoring.

- One really good list was the one over ideas for posts: tell a personal story, describe a historical event, review a book/movie/app, comment on a powerful quote, let a great photograph inspire you, comment on something in the news, report on an interesting conversation, provide a step-by-step explanation for how to do something, provide a list of resources, answer your readers' questions, make a seemingly overwhelming task simple, explain the rationale behind a decision, write a guide to something popular.

- Hyatt highlighted, with painful accuracy, the importance of posting frequently and consistently for a long period of time. Many of the tips in the book are about how to make it easier to do that, but the core of the work is just showing up and doing the work, day after day, for years. This is something I definitely struggle with myself, as I quickly get distracted by new projects and bored when I don't achieve the results I want overnight.

- There were a few examples of how preparation can go a long way (and scale really well) — creating a good About page that you can link to from your social media profiles, preparing a list of questions for interviewers, handing conference announcers an introduction to read when they introduce you, etc. These are all great tips that I will be implementing.
