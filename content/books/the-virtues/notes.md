the virtues, by craig a. boyd

One big takeaway for me was that virtue (and vice) is self-reinforcing. The more you develop it, the easier it becomes to act in accordance with it, and the more you act in accordance with it, the more it develops.

- Virtues are "excellent and stable qualities of the soul that enables a person to act well". It is a character trait but not one that is fixed, you're not born a virtuous person but rather can become one by developing and improving virtue over time. Virtues are not just about doing the right thing but also about developing good character traits that make it easier to do the right thing consistently. The virtuous person enjoys doing the right thing and finds it easy to do so, while the vicious person finds it difficult and unpleasant to do the right thing.

- Vices mirrors virtues in that they are "stable qualities of the soul", only bad ones. You expect a liar to lie often and easily, the truly vicious to be very good at lying and to enjoy doing it.

- The author kept pushing the idea that virtue doesn't have to be moral, but it could be productive (chefs, for example) or intellectual (scientists, for example). This didn't really strike a chord with me, as I think that the moral aspect of virtue is what makes it interesting and important. He quoted Aristotle as saying that virtues help us "to live, think, and do well". I suspect Aristotle meant something more along the lines of what Jeff Olson said in The Slight Edge: that attitude turns into actions, which turns into results, which turns into a lifestyle.

- What makes the virtues good and vices bad is how they affect society, the person, and the person's standing in society. They act as proxies for behaviour that optimizes for either short-term, shallow good or long-term, substantial good. As hypersocial animals, this is foundational to our nature.

- There is a huge component of cultural relativity to virtues and vices, but there are also some that are more universal. For example, honesty is generally considered a virtue across cultures, while lying is considered a vice. However, the specific virtues and vices that are valued can vary greatly between cultures.

- The author mentioned the Buddhistic concept of "muditā", which I looked up: "Muditā is a dharmic concept of joy, particularly an especially sympathetic or vicarious joy—the pleasure that comes from delighting in other people's well-being".

- One thing I considered was the similarity between courage and temperance. Both are about finding a balance between extremes: courage is about finding the right amount of fear and confidence, while temperance is about finding the right amount of pleasure and pain. Both virtues require a kind of self-control and moderation.

- It's a shame how badly Christianity and Islam set back the development of virtue ethics. The Greeks got the ball rolling, but then the Catholics and Muslims just had to shoehorn everything into their ill-fitting, dogmatic framework.

- Virtues are self-fulfilling, in that they lead to a good life, for both individuals and society, but also because they are their own reward.

- I lean more towards virtue ethics than consequentialism or deontology, because it strikes a better balance between contextual nuance and practical guidance. All are tools for moral reasoning, but virtue ethics seems to better capture the complexity of human character and the reinforcement loop between actions and character development. It allows for a more holistic approach to morality, where the focus is on becoming a good person rather than just doing good things, which feels more sustainable in the long run.

- I see some strong similarities between virtue ethics and fitness. Both are about developing good habits and character traits that lead to a better life, and in both cases, the process is self-reinforcing. Just as developing virtue makes it easier to act virtuously, developing good fitness habits makes it easier to maintain a healthy lifestyle. Starting from zero, exercise is painful and difficult, healthy eating is bland and unsatisfying, and the benefits are invisible. But as you develop those habits, they become easier and even enjoyable. Most fit people honestly enjoy working out and eating healthy, and they find it difficult to do otherwise. The same is true for virtues: as you develop them, they become easier and more enjoyable to practice, and it becomes difficult to act in ways that are contrary to those virtues.

- Also see some strong similarities with veganism. A common complaint is that vegan food isn't very tasty, but taste is malleable, and as they develop on a vegan diet, people often find that they enjoy the taste of vegan food more and more.
