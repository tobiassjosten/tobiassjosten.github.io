While organized work in groups can be powerful, there are several pitfalls to be aware of. Dominica Degrandis outlines the most vicious ones, in terms of effectiveness, and prescribes solutions for detecting and mitigating them all.

The main theme of the book is that by visualizing our work, it makes it easier for us humans to understand and manage it. This is especially important in complex systems, where there are many moving parts and dependencies. By making work visible, we can identify bottlenecks, reduce waste, and improve flow.

I loved how Dominica showed a myriad of different designs for boards and visualizations, and how to use them to expose different issues in the workflow. The book is very practical and actionable, with lots of examples to inspire one to experiment with different ways of making work visible.

An interesting aside was how all of these techniques and principles are applicable to my personal life as well. I've dabbled in personal kanban boards for a while, but this gave me a lot of new ideas for how to run our household more effectively.

- Very much focused on software development and the challenges of managing work in that context, but the principles can be applied to any field.

- Dominica Degrandis boils down the ideal workflow system for software development to five core principles: 1) make work visible, 2) limit work in progress, 3) measure and manage flow, 4) prioritize effectively, and 5) make adjustments based on learnings from feedback and metrics..

- On the flip side, the book also identifies five "thieves of time" that can prevent teams from achieving optimal flow: 1) too much work in progress, 2) unknown dependencies, 3) unplanned work, 4) conflicting priorities, and 5) neglected work.

Too much work in progress:
- Limiting work in progress is crucial for improving flow and reducing the time it takes to complete work. It's counter-intuitive when you're in the weeds of a project, but the more work you have in progress, the longer it takes to finish anything. Context switching and multitasking are killers of productivity. And as soon as you leave work half-finished, to focus on something new, not only do you lose momentum on the original task, but it adds delay to delivering value to customers.
- Learning to say "no" helps reduce WIP. It's not limiting, but liberating. It allows you to focus on completing work and delivering value, rather than getting bogged down in a never-ending stream of tasks and spreading yourself too thin.
- Attacking if from a multitude of angles, the author really drives home the point that limiting WIP is one of the most powerful things you can do to improve flow and reduce time to value. I'm convinced that this is a fundamental principle of effective work, however counter-intuitive it may seem at first.

Unknown dependencies:
- There are three types of dependencies: architecture (a change somewhere affects functionality elsewhere), expertise (requiring input from a SME or authority), and activity (the required sequencing of events).
- Troy Magennis showed through boolean logic how each added dependency exponentially increases the number of failure points or delays, leading to a doubling of the time it takes to complete work for every added dependency. This is a powerful argument for reducing dependencies as much as possible.

Unplanned work:
- Plan for unplanned work by building in buffer time and having a process for handling it when it arises. This can help prevent unplanned work from derailing your progress and ensure that you can still meet your commitments.
- Tracking planned work will allow you to plan for the unplanned by reserving a margin of time for it.

Conflicting priorities:
- Teams working together with different (or even just uncoordinated) priorities can lead to delays and inefficiencies. It's important to align priorities across teams and ensure that everyone is working towards the same goals. This requires getting clear on the overall vision and strategy, and then breaking it down into concrete goals that everyone can rally around.

Neglected work:
- Neglected work can lead to technical debt and other issues that can slow down progress over time.
- What Donald Reinertsen dubbed "zombie projects" are low-value projects that continue to consume resources and attention without delivering meaningful value, dragging down higher-value projects by their presence. It's important to identify and eliminate these zombie projects to free up resources for more valuable work.

Measurements to consider tracking:
- Work in progress:
- Cycle time:
- Time in process: identify zombie projects and make informed decisions about whether to continue investing in them or to cut your losses and move on.
- Unplanned work: how many work items are planned vs unplanned, and the delta for how this changes over time.
- Oldest WIP: highlight neglected work and identify potential bottlenecks in the workflow.
- Flow efficiency: work / (wait + work) * 100.

- The majority of people understands better from visual information than from text. By making work visible, it becomes easier for whole teams to understand their flow of work, identify bottlenecks, and make informed decisions about how to improve their processes.

- In order to balance what teams are working on, work items could be categorized by the type of work they represent, such as Unplanned, Maintenance, Business, Team Improvement, etc, visualized by color on a board. The team should develop this categorization themselves, to make sure all of their work is represented and to get buy-in from everyone.

- The book lists many different exercises for visualizing work, such as "demand analysis", "card design", "workflow mapping", etc. While I enjoy the thoroughness of the book, much of it felt quite basic for someone who's been working in software development for a while.

- One useful visualization for exposing the issue of having too much work in progress, is to create "swimlanes" for work on the board, based on who requested it. The author suggests using the categories silver bullets (urgent, initiated from an executive), business (normal features and bugs from regular stakeholders), and teamwork (technical debt, security, maintenance, etc). I can see this being a powerful tool to understand the distribution of work and to have conversations about whether the current distribution is healthy or not.

- Other uses of swimlanes:
  - To visualize dependencies, create one lane for work with dependencies to other teams, and one lane for work without dependencies. This can help teams identify potential bottlenecks.
  - To visualize sidelines, create one lane for planned work, and one lane for interruptions/unplanned work. This can help teams understand how much of their time is being spent on unplanned work and identify opportunities to reduce it.

- The use of visualization to communicate with stakeholders and dependants is a powerful tool and reminds me of the book Product Roadmaps Relaunched. These two can make an effective combo to upgrade one's project management and communication skills.

- Repetitive tasks also very much affect WIP and should be visualized and tracked as well.

- The focus on limiting WIP reminds me of the books Essentialism, The One Thing, and Deep Work, which all advocate for a more focused and intentional approach to work. Counter-intuitive as it may seem, doing less leads to getting more done, better and less stressful.

- An aging report is a visualization that shows how long work items have been in progress. Comparing this to the average can show which items are taking longer than usual and may be at risk of becoming neglected or "zombie projects".

- The repeated mention of pomodoro technique as a way to limit WIP and improve focus is interesting. I've dimisssed it in the past as a gimmick, but it seems to have some merit as a tool for managing work and improving flow. It might be worth giving it a try to see if it helps with focus and productivity.

- I liked the addition of the meetings chapter. One gem I'm taking away from it is the principle of having everyone update the board before 9 am (or whenever the standup begins). Then focus can be on discussing risks and problems, rather than a tedious status update.

- A small but concrete insight is to include weekends in reports. This both leads to more accurate data and aligns better with customer expectations.

- I'm now convinced that the team should be the smallest unit of measurement and the focus should be on the work done, not on individual performance. This is a powerful shift in perspective that can lead to more effective teamwork and overall improved performance, since it encourages cooperation and shared responsibility for outcomes.

- I would love to apply the principle of limiting WIP to the various roles and responsibilities I have in my life, such as work, family, hobbies, etc. It's not as easy to implement as it is in a software development context, however, where the work is so clearly defined, but that might just mean that I have to structure my other areas better and work to implement it over time.
