I've spent decades working in and around software teams, and one of the single most persistent problem I've seen is teams drowning in work they don't fully see. Dominica Degrandis wrote this book to address exactly that, and her prescription is deceptively simple: if you want to manage work, make it visible first.

The book identifies five "thieves of time" that quietly sabotage teams: too much work in progress, unknown dependencies, unplanned work, conflicting priorities, and neglected work. None of these are surprising individually. What Degrandis does well is show how they compound. Each dependency you add roughly doubles your chance of delay. Each half-finished task lingers, eating context-switch costs. Each zombie project siphons energy from work that matters. She builds a convincing case that these problems are systemic, not personal, and that visibility is the first step toward fixing them.

The argument for limiting work in progress was the part I found most compelling. It's a lesson I keep relearning: doing less leads to finishing more. Degrandis approaches this from multiple angles, queuing theory, flow metrics, practical board designs, and by the end I was genuinely persuaded that WIP limits belong in every team's toolkit. The connection to [Essentialism](/books/essentialism/), [The One Thing](/books/the-one-thing/), and [Deep Work](/books/deep-work/) is obvious, and it's encouraging to see the same principle validated from such different directions.

I loved the variety of board designs and visualization techniques she presents. Swimlanes for exposing who requested work, lanes for separating planned from unplanned tasks, aging reports for flagging neglected items. These are practical and immediately applicable. The suggestion to have everyone update the board before standup, so the meeting focuses on risks instead of status updates, is a small change I plan to implement.

One thing that surprised me was how transferable these ideas are to personal life. I've experimented with personal kanban boards before, but this book gave me new ideas for running a household with less chaos and more intention. The principle of limiting WIP applies as much to family logistics as to sprint planning.

If you lead a team or manage projects and don't yet have a shared way to see your work, start here. Experienced kanban practitioners will find the material familiar, but even then the variety of board designs and exercises makes it worth a skim.

The book left me wanting to redesign my own boards, both at work and at home, and that's about the best endorsement I have for a book on process.
