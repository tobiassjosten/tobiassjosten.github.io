The book boils down to this: success and failure are the result of small, everyday choices that compound over time. Making slightly better choices consistently leads to success, while making slightly worse choices consistently leads to failure. It's not about big, dramatic actions, but the small, seemingly insignificant ones that add up over time.

The obvious conclusion is that it's critical to become intentional about the small choices we make every day, as they have a profound impact on our long-term outcomes, and to do so as soon as possible, since the effects are cumulative.

- His own journey from "beach bum" to successful business owner is inspiring and — despite being a sample size of one — lends weight to his advice.

- One thing that stayed with me is the cascading effect of philosphy affecting attitude, attitude affecting actions, actions affecting results, and results affecting your overall life. I'm allergic to the idea of "positive thinking" as a cure-all, or the ludicrous notion of manifestation, but here Olson gives a rational explanation of how it could work (without the woo-woo, thankfully).

- Once Olson pointed out the concept of "quantum leap", I've realized how prevalent this line of thinking is in our culture. We are conditioned to expect success in the form of overnight transformations, viral sensations, and sudden windfalls. This mindset can be detrimental, as it overlooks the importance of consistent, incremental progress.
  - A personal example of this is my proclivity for powerlifting over long-distance running. I much rather prefer one, big, dramatic lift, than the slow grind of kilometer after kilometer.

- It was eye-opening to realize how the effects of small, seemingly insignificant actions can accumulate over time to produce significant results, and how this principle applies even (or especially) when we're not intentional about our actions, in which case it most likely works against our long-term goals.

- Eating is an example of The Slight Edge in action. A single unhealthy meal won't make you fat, just as a single healthy meal won't make you fit. But consistently making poor food choices will lead to weight gain over time, while consistently making healthy choices will lead to improved fitness and well-being.

- Olson lists three reasons why people fail to make the better, everyday choices that lead to success:
  - They're easy to do, and just as easy not to do. This makes them prone to procrastination.
  - The results are invisible. They don't produce immediate gratification, so it's easy to overlook their importance.
  - They seem insignificant. It's easy to dismiss small actions as unimportant, leading to a lack of motivation to follow through.

- While the book itself is mostly rational and free of fluff, Olson does veer into the realm of woo-woo but mainly by references to recommended books and other thinkers, so it's easy to skip over those parts.

- Once you have taken a more intentional approach to life, time starts working FOR you, instead of against you. The compounding effect of small, positive actions leads to exponential growth and improvement over time.
