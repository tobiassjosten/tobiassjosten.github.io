This is one of the best and most thought-provoking books I've read in a long time. Blackburn delves into the inherently delicate topic of ethics in a clear and accessible manner, making complex ideas understandable without oversimplifying them.

My main takeaway is this: Ethics is the glue that holds a society together and enables people to cooperate to achieve both common and individual goals. If the book Sapiens explained the power of humans in groups, this book explains how it is even possible to harness that power.

Our species can't really work with absolutes; we look to our environment and experiences to determine what is normal and what stands out. This means that standards are extremely fluid and flexible, arbitrary even. So why not take a holistic approach to it, and work systematically and intentionally to define the most optimal standard possible? That is the role of ethics.

- Humans have an innate tendency not only to prefer certain actions but also to judge them as right or wrong. When something is sufficiently important, we expect others to do the same.

- In the West today, there is a strong emphasis on individual rights and freedoms. However, Blackburn argues that for a society to function effectively, something needs to uphold the ethical standards that enable these rights and freedoms. If we do not collectively take responsibility for maintaining ethical norms — and do not want to surrender them to a tyrannical regime that enforces them for us — society will collapse. Someone has to take responsibility for upholding these ethical standards.

- While humans have a natural inclination towards ethical behavior, it is often uninformed and inconsistent; immature. We need to engage in ethical reasoning to determine what is right and wrong in various situations, and to develop strong and consistent ethical principles. The challenge lies in our tendency to avoid or flee from difficult ethical questions, preferring to stick to familiar but potentially flawed moral intuitions.

Blackburn lists seven major threats to thinking about ethics clearly:
1. The threat of the death of God, because disconnecting ethics from religion would devalue faith. Yet, religion isn't sufficiently consistent to provide a solid ethical foundation, nor would following religious rules blindly lead to satisfying ethical behavior.
2. The threat of relativism, because if all ethical views are equally valid, any one set of ethical standards loses its value. Yet, there are universal ethical topics (albeit with varying specific values) that transcend cultures — truth-telling, promise-giving, property, violence and sex regulation, treatment of the weaker — indicating a shared human need for ethical standards. Respecting other cultures' ethical standards is important, but it should not come at the cost of moral blindness or the inability to criticize harmful practices. We must also consider the victims of those practices, who deserve equal or greater consideration than the perpetrators.
3. The threat of egoism, because some might argue that self-interest is the primary motivator for human behavior, rendering ethics irrelevant or dubious, since you could never be sure that anyone is acting ethically rather than out of self-interest. Yet, others show us who they are by their actions, and there are plenty of examples of people acting against their self-interest for ethical reasons. Arguing that honor, altruism, and integrity are aspects of self-interest moves the goalposts and kidnaps the word self-interest for whatever the agent is concerned about.
4. The threat of evolutionary theory, because if our ethical behavior is merely a product of evolutionary processes, it could be seen as arbitrary or contingent, undermining its authority. Yet, while evolution may explain the origins of certain ethical behaviors, it doesn't invalidate them. Humans having evolved to be cooperative and empathetic doesn't mean that these traits are any less real or valuable. Evolutionary explanations can coexist with ethical reasoning, and understanding our evolutionary history can even enhance our appreciation for the ethical principles that have emerged from it.
5. The threat of determinism and futility, because if we are determined by our biology and environment, it could lead to a sense of futility in trying to make ethical choices. Yet, even if our actions are influenced by various factors, we still have the capacity for reflection and choice. Determinism doesn't necessarily negate free will or moral responsibility; it simply complicates our understanding of how we make decisions. We can still strive to make ethical choices and hold ourselves accountable for them, even in a deterministic framework.
6. The threat of unreasonable demands, because if the conclusion of ethical reasoning is that we must make unreasonable sacrifices for the sake of others, it could lead to a rejection of ethics altogether. Yet, ethics doesn't necessarily demand extreme self-sacrifice, it's about reasonable expectations from our fellow man. While adhering to more stringent ethical standards might be admirable, it's not demanded of us.
7. The threat of false consciousness, because ethics can be (and has many times been) hijacked by a ruling class to justify their actions and maintain their power. Yet, while ethics can be misused, it doesn't mean that ethical reasoning itself is invalid. We must be vigilant against such abuses and strive to develop ethical principles that promote justice and equality.

- One thing the book didn't directly point out, but which I found interesting when reflecting on what was being said between the lines, was the humanistic version of a judgemental god. Instead of a supernatural being who judges us, we have a society that judges us. As hypersocial creatures, that can be just as scary, just as powerful, as any imaginary, omnipotent being overseeing our correct behavior.

- The tradition following Aristoteles focuses on the development of good character traits (virtues, e.g. virtue ethics) as the basis for ethical behavior. The idea is that by cultivating virtues such as courage, honesty, and temperance, individuals will naturally make ethical decisions. This approach emphasizes the importance of moral education and personal growth in becoming a virtuous person. I am personally very attracted to this approach, as it seems to be more holistic and focused on the development of the individual, rather than just following rules or maximizing utility.

- Kant turned against the Golden Rule and developed the Categorical Imperative, which is a principle that states that one should only act according to maxims that can be universally applied. In other words, you should only do something if you would be okay with everyone else doing it as well. This approach emphasizes the importance of consistency and universality in ethical decision-making, without the pitfalls of generalizing the strategy of asocial individuals to the whole of humanity.

- I found the idea of using the prisoner's dilemma in reverse, coupled with evolutionary theory, to be a compelling argument for the development of ethical behavior. Cooperative people will result in more offspring, which will lead to the proliferation of cooperative traits in the population. This creates a positive feedback loop where ethical behavior becomes more common and advantageous over time.

- After learning about the arguments for and against, I find myself disliking utalitarianism. The idea of equalizing someone's suffering with someone else's pleasure seems to be a very flawed way of looking at the world. It also seems to be a very reductionist approach to ethics, as it reduces all ethical considerations to a single metric of pleasure and pain.

- Freedom is a powerful word, which can be used to justify a wide range of principles. However, it is important to recognize that most freedoms come at the expense of another. For example, the freedom to express oneself may conflict with the freedom from harm or discrimination. It is crucial to strike a balance between different freedoms and to consider the potential consequences of prioritizing one freedom over another.

- I absolutely loved how Blackburn dared to take on the topics of abortion and assisted suicide, which is often considered a taboo subject in ethical discussions. He approached it with nuance and sensitivity, acknowledging the complexity of the issue and the various perspectives involved. This willingness to engage with difficult topics is commendable and adds depth to the book's exploration of ethics.
