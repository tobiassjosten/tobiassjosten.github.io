Learning Domain-Driven Design, by Vladik Khononov

DDD is a software development methodology that emphasizes the importance of understanding the business domain and modeling it in code. It encourages developers to work closely with domain experts to create a shared understanding of the problem space and to design software that reflects that understanding. DDD consists of a set of principles and practices that help developers create software that is more maintainable, flexible, and aligned with the needs of the business.

- The book is a personal narrative of Vladik's journey learning DDD, and it's clear that he has a deep passion for the subject, a desire to share his insights with others, and the scars to show having gone through the process of learning the methodology himself.

- DDD puts the business domain front and center, as it's the reason we develop software in the first place. However, business isn't the driver in DDD, but rather the navigator. While our business colleagues and customers might be subject matter experts, they often lack the systematic thinking and technical expertise to design software. DDD is about bridging that gap, and it's the developers who are responsible for doing the heavy lifting of modeling the domain in code, and in many cases guiding the business in understanding their own domain better.

- There are two tracks to applying DDD: the strategic track, which focuses on understanding the business domain and modeling it effectively, and the tactical track, which focuses on the technical implementation of DDD principles in code. Both tracks are important for mastering DDD, and they complement each other.

- DDD categorizes subdomains into three types: core, supporting, and generic. The core domain is the most critical part of the business and should receive the most attention and resources. Supporting subdomains are important but does not offer any competitive advantage. Generic subdomains are common across many businesses and can often be outsourced or handled with off-the-shelf solutions. While artificially clean, I love this separation because it helps to prioritize efforts and resources, and to focus on what really matters for the business. It also helps to avoid getting bogged down in details that may not be relevant to the core domain, and to make informed decisions about where to invest time and energy.
