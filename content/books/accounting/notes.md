This was a surprisingly engaging read on a topic that often feels dry and technical. The book goes beyond the mechanics of accounting to explore its evolution, the judgment calls involved, and the challenges of standardization across different countries. It deepened my appreciation for accounting as both a science and an art, highlighting its critical role in business and finance.

- I quite enjoyed the historical context provided in the book, which helped me understand how accounting practices have evolved over time and the factors that have influenced these changes.

- The author seems like the perfect person to write this book, given his obvious passion for the subject (talking about the satisfaction of reconciling accounts made me smile).

- It was interesting to learn how much of an art form accounting is, with many decisions relying on judgment calls rather than strict rules. One concrete example: how assets are valued and the different methods used (selling price, market price, original cost, business worth).

- The global perspective on accounting standards and practices highlighted the challenges of achieving consistency across different countries and cultures.

- Having worked with IFRS data, I enjoyed the deep dive into the history and principles behind these standards and how they compare to others like GAAP.

- While I understand the distinction between assets and staff, and why training courses are considered expenses rather than investments, I'm not quite sure I agree with the clear-cut separation. In today's knowledge-driven economy, investing in employee development seems more akin to building an asset than incurring a mere expense, and the control aspect feels increasingly blurred.

- I chuckled at the quote about "lies, damned lies, statistics, and financial statements," which captures the skepticism many have towards accounting figures. It serves as a reminder that while accounting aims for objectivity, it is still subject to interpretation and manipulation.

- I liked the idea of budgeting as a tool not only for finance but also to force executives to think through their plans and strategies in detail and to hold themselves accountable.
