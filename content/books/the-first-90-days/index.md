I wish I had read this book a long time ago. I would have avoided so many rookie mistakes as a new leader, from bringing habits that didn't fit my new context to misreading what kind of situation I was walking into.

The First 90 Days is a framework for the critical transition period when you step into a new leadership role. Michael D. Watkins structures the book around ten core skills: preparing yourself, accelerating your learning, matching strategy to situation, securing early wins, negotiating success, achieving alignment, building your team, creating coalitions, keeping your balance, and accelerating everyone. These skills form the backbone of a practical roadmap for getting up to speed quickly without stumbling into common traps.

What stuck with me most was the STARS framework for diagnosing business situations: start-up, turnaround, accelerated growth, realignment, or sustaining success. Each requires a different approach, and I realized how my own leadership style could improve by adapting to the context. The book also made me think harder about how I make decisions. Do I consult and decide? Do I build consensus? I used to approach this instinctively. Now I do so more consciously.

The book is full of checklists and tables. Some are genuinely useful. Others feel like they're there because the author thought every idea needed a checklist. I also wanted more depth on organizational design. Watkins repeatedly calls this out as a critical skill and a common gap in leaders, but stops short of offering concrete advice on how to get better at it. You'll need to look elsewhere for that.

The emphasis on relationships stands out. Success in a new role depends on building trust and rapport quickly, not only with your team and superiors but also horizontally with peers and other stakeholders. This advice is mindful of the many different dimensions of leadership, and the book does a good job of balancing them all.

The advice is so clear and actionable that I finished reading and felt like applying for a new role, only to put these ideas into practice. Much like [The Manager's Path](/books/the-managers-path/), this book brings different leadership styles to the surface and helps you reflect on your own approach. I came away with a sharper sense of how to adapt and where I need to grow.

If you're stepping into a new leadership position, read this book before you start. If you've been in a leadership role for a while and never had formal training, read it anyway. If you're in an individual contributor role with no interest in leadership, skip it. There's nothing here for you.
