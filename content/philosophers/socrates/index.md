Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher widely regarded as one of the founders of Western philosophy. Unlike other philosophers of his time, Socrates wrote nothing himself; instead, our knowledge of his teachings comes primarily through the dialogues of his student Plato and the writings of Xenophon.

Socrates developed the Socratic method, a form of cooperative argumentative dialogue that uses questioning to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate ideas. He famously claimed that "the unexamined life is not worth living" and devoted himself to examining the beliefs and assumptions of his fellow Athenians.

His relentless questioning of authority and conventional wisdom eventually led to his trial and execution in 399 BCE on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth of Athens. Rather than fleeing or compromising his principles, Socrates accepted the death sentence, drinking hemlock and becoming a martyr for philosophy and free inquiry.
